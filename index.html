Why MAT prep is different

The MAT is not just another maths exam.

The MAT tests whether you can think clearly with unfamiliar problems, not just whether you know the syllabus. Many students know the A-Level content, but struggle when the question is phrased in a new or subtle way.

My tutoring focuses on bridging that gap: turning school maths knowledge into flexible problem-solving ability.

  • Learning how to start difficult problems when the method is not obvious.
  • Building fluency with algebra, functions, sequences, geometry and proof.
  • Practising multiple-choice questions without relying on guessing.
  • Writing clear solutions to longer questions under time pressure.
  • Reviewing mistakes carefully so weak patterns are fixed, not repeated.
Teaching method

A focused approach to MAT preparation.

Lessons are structured around carefully chosen problems. The aim is not to rush through as many questions as possible, but to understand exactly how to think when the route is unclear.

Diagnose weak points

We identify which areas are causing difficulty: algebraic manipulation, problem interpretation, proof, timing, or confidence with unfamiliar questions.

Build problem-solving tools

I teach general techniques: trying examples, spotting structure, simplifying the problem, working backwards, and choosing useful notation.

Practise under exam conditions

As the test approaches, we move towards timed practice, past MAT questions and detailed review of solution strategy.

The goal is for students to become calmer and more systematic when faced with a hard question. That is often what separates a good A-Level mathematician from a strong MAT candidate.

What lessons cover

Content and skills.

Core MAT topics

Algebra, functions, graphs, sequences, coordinate geometry, trigonometry, differentiation, integration, proof and logical reasoning.

Exam technique

Multiple-choice strategy, time management, avoiding common traps, presenting long solutions, and reviewing past-paper attempts effectively.

Problem-solving habits

Learning when to test small cases, draw a diagram, introduce notation, rearrange the question, or search for hidden structure.

Oxbridge preparation

For suitable students, lessons can also include interview-style mathematical discussion and preparation for explaining ideas aloud.
